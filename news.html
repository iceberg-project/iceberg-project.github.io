SealNet paper published!

Posted on January 02, 2020 by Brad Spitzbart

The first ICEBERG related domain science paper has been published in Remote Sensing of Environment.
Click the title for open-access to the full manuscript until Feb 19, 2020.

SealNet: A fully-automated pack-ice seal detection pipeline for sub-meter satellite imagery
Authors: B.C. Goncalves, B. Spitzbart, H.J. Lynch

Highlights:
- First automated system for surveying seals using satellite imagery
- Finds 30% of seals while only generating less than 2 false-positives per correct detection
- Over 10X faster than an experienced human observer using a single GPU

Check out ICEBERG at AGU!

Posted on November 1, 2018 by Heather Lynch

Samira Daneshgar Asl from UCSB will be giving a poster on Wednesday.
Abstract Title: High-Resolution Satellite Imagery Monitoring of Greenland Ice Sheet Supraglacial Hydrologic Features
Final Paper Number: C33D-2460
Session Date and Time: Wednesday, 12 December 2018; 13:40 - 18:00
Session Number and Title: C33D: Glacier Monitoring from in Situ and Remotely Sensed Observations Posters
Location: Convention Center; Hall A-C (Poster Hall)

Mike MacFerrin and Mike Willis from UC Boulder will be giving a poster on Wednesday.
Abstract Title: Toward fully-automated geolocation of historic airborne imagery to construct an 80-year time series of Antarctic outlet glacier extents and volumes
Session Date and Time: Wednesday, 12 December 2018; 13:40 - 18:00
Session Number and Title: C33D: Glacier Monitoring from in Situ and Remotely Sensed Observations Posters
Location: Convention Center; Hall A-C (Poster Hall).
Digital scans of historic airborne and/or satellite photos have been used to successfully interpret past extents of glaciers and ice sheets over mountainous terrain (e.g. Kjeldsen et al., 2015; Kääb et al., 2018). The use of historic aerial photos allows modern researchers to look further into the past than is possible with contemporary satellite measurements alone. Large datasets containing many images require significant labor to accurately locate ground-control points in each image before further processing can be performed. A processing pipeline combining several modified algorithms from computer vision can automate image geo-location and ortho-rectification of otherwise poorly geolocated airborne images. Using an affine-, scale- and rotation-invariant image matching algorithm (Yu & Morel, 2011), distinct “keypoint” features are identified in historic airborne images and matched to similar keypoints found in modern WorldView imagery. A monte-carlo filtering algorithm selects a set of matching keypoints that maintain consistent image topology, allowing the automatic generation of geolocated ground control points without individual manual selection. Here we present preliminary results of this processing pipeline applied to the US Navy’s trimetrogon aerial photography campaign which collected more than 3,300 images over Antarctica during the 1930-50’s, in order to construct a glacial history of outlet glaciers covering much of West Antarctica’s peripheral regions. The algorithm is being developed as an open-source processing pipeline for public release to the research community, with initial results presented here.
